Overview

What Are Robotaxis?

A look into the future reality of a taxi industry without steering wheels.

Self-Driving Cars, Available On Demand

As technology continues to evolve, most automotive companies are finding ways to incorporate autonomous vehicles (AVs) into their fleets. AVs are cars that use cameras, sensors, and AI to navigate surroundings and “automate” the driving process to varying degrees of human involvement. SAE International, a professional association that focuses on developing technical standards across different industries, defines the levels of driving automation as a range from 0-5 (no automation to full automation). The type of AV that we will be focused on in this report, colloquially known as robotaxis, are driverless vehicles that fall under Level 4 on the SAE scale. An important distinction to make is that while robotaxis operate without human drivers, they are not technically considered to be “fully automated” vehicles. This is because robotaxis are limited by “geofencing,” meaning they can only operate in specific mapped areas and under good weather conditions. Despite not reaching the Level 5 threshold, robotaxis are presently being deployed commercially through ride-hailing services in certain cities as on-demand AVs.

  • Like legacy services such as Uber and Lyft, users order rides through a mobile app.
  • Vehicles are equiped with sensors, cameras, and artificial intelignece, which eliminates the need for present human drivers
  • Companies such as Waymo and Zoox control fleets of robotaxis.
  • Early stage pilot programs already in deployment, currently available to users in select cities across the globe.

Analysis

Impact on the Transportation Industry

How autonomous vehicles are changing the economics, safety, and operations of urban transportation.

Advantages

Key benefits that autonomous vehicles bring to the transportation sector.

  • Enhanced safety - 94% of crashes are a result of human error.
  • Significantly reduced travel expenses by eliminating the cost of human drivers.
  • Increased efficiency through optimized routes resulting in reduced traffic congestion.
  • Increased accessibility for those who suffer from social anxiety that may otherwise avoid taking a taxi or digital ride-hailing service due to the human driver component.

Disadvantages

Challenges and risks that the industry must take into account prior to large-scale adoption.

  • Current large-scale adoption remains largely impractical today, with Level 4 AVs frequently requiring human intervention in cases of unexpected hazards or edge cases.
  • Older age individuals perceive robotaxis as “inferior drivers”, believing their driving or that of humans in general to be superior to a robot.
  • Responsibility of accidents shifts to remote human workers, which may result in hazardous situations in the event of an overwhelmed employee.
  • As responsibility shifts, the legal system may find it difficult to assign liability in the event of an accident.

Market Leaders

Who's Building the Future?

The companies and products at the forefront of large-scale robotaxi commercialization.

Alphabet / Google

Waymo

Waymo, a subsidiary of Alphabet Inc. (Google’s parent company), is a company that has led the commercial robotaxi industry. Waymo has managed to bring fully autonomous robotaxis to the widespread market quicker than competitors thanks to retrofitting (adding new technology to older systems).

Level 4 Autonomy Multi-City Fully Driverless
Amazon

Zoox

Zoox, a subsidiary of Amazon, is a company that focuses more on building fully autonomous vehicles from the ground-up as opposed to retrofitting. Although this strategy has slowed their rate of commercial implementation—as most of their products are still in testing stages—it has allowed the company to pivot their focus towards a futuristic experience for customers.

Timeline of Level 4 Use Cases

Based on McKinsey’s survey asking global decision makers their expected year of mass deployment, averaged across multiple world regions (Europe, North America, China, Asia-Pacific).

2028 Estimate

Level 3 Highway Pilot (Consumer Vehicles, up to 120km/hr)

Conditionaly automated highway pilot systems allowing speeds up to 120 km/h are expected to see mass deployment, sparking the transition to a hands-free driving experience.

2029 Estimate

Driverless on Highway, Hub to Hub (Trucks)

The adoption of fully driverless trucks on select hub-to-hub highway routes is expected to reach mass deployment, paving the way for full urban trucking in the coming years.

2030 Estimate

Level 4 or Level 5 Robotaxi in Geofenced Urban Areas

Completely driverless taxi services will reach mass deployment, revolutionizing the way we look at ride-hailing services.

2032 Estimate

Level 4 Urban Pilot (Consumer Vehicles) & Full Driverless Trucks

Increased autonomous capabilities reach the market, allowing for consumers and trucking companies to experience the benefits of high automation.

Source: McKinsey Center for Future Mobility, 2025 estimate.

Valid Skepticism

These projections can write a completely different narrative since the 2030 figure is a 1-year setback from the original survey taken in 2023 that projected large-scale adoption by 2029. (Kelkar et al., 2026).
